<!--[H2]--><section id="ref283775" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">Traditional history and first contact</h2>
<p>Their traditional history describes their origins in terms of waves of migration that culminated in the arrival of a “great fleet” in the 14th century from <span id="ref180279"></span>Hawaiki, a mythical land usually identified as Tahiti. This historical account provides the basis for traditional Maori social organization and is generally supported by archaeological discoveries, which have dated Maori arrival in New Zealand to about 1300 <span class="text-smallcaps">ce</span>. Members of each tribe (<em><span id="ref794782"></span>iwi</em>) recognized a common ancestry (which might be traced through either or both parents) and common <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/allegiance" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="allegiance">allegiance</a> to a chief or chiefs (<em><span id="ref794785"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/arika" class="md-crosslink">ariki</a></em>). Traditionally, at the day-to-day level, the most important social groups were the <em><span id="ref794786"></span>hapuu</em> (subtribe), which was the primary landholding group and the one within which marriage was preferred, and the <em>whaanau</em>, or <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/extended-family" class="md-crosslink autoxref">extended family</a>.</p><!--[P2]--><!--[AM2]--><span id="AM2"></span><!--[MOD2]--><span id="MOD2"></span>
<p>This social order was in force when <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Abel-Tasman" class="md-crosslink">Abel Tasman</a>, the first European contact, arrived off the coast of New Zealand in December 1642. He did battle with a group of Maori on <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/South-Island-New-Zealand" class="md-crosslink autoxref">South Island</a> and left the area largely unexplored. In 1769–70 Captain <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/James-Cook" class="md-crosslink">James Cook</a> circumnavigated the two major islands and wrote about the intelligence of the Maori and the suitability of New Zealand for colonization. Whalers, sealers, and other Europeans seeking profit were initially welcomed by the Maori. With the introduction of muskets, disease, Western agricultural methods, and missionaries, Maori <a href="https://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/culture" class="md-dictionary-link md-dictionary-tt-off" data-term="culture">culture</a> and social structure began to disintegrate. By the late 1830s New Zealand had been joined to Europe, and European settlers landed by the score.</p><!--[P3]--><!--[AM3]--><div class="ora-tv-container"><span id="ora_bf98bb76-ccd0-42bd-a1da-23d47b3b4ae8"></span></div><span id="AM3"></span><!--[MOD3]--><span id="MOD3"></span></section>
<!--[H3]--><section id="ref283776" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">The rise of the <span id="ref180281"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maori-King-Movement" class="md-crosslink">King Movement</a></h2>
<p>After the British assumed formal control of New Zealand in 1840, European settlement and government began to alarm the Maori, especially in <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/North-Island" class="md-crosslink autoxref">North Island</a>. In 1845 some Maori chieftains began ravaging the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Bay-of-Islands" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Bay of Islands</a> and other areas of the far north (in what has sometimes been called the <span id="ref180280"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maori-Wars" class="md-crosslink">First Maori War</a>), and they were not finally suppressed until 1847, by colonial forces under Governor <span id="ref794795"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/George-Grey" class="md-crosslink">Sir George Grey</a>. His victories brought a peace that lasted from 1847 to 1860.</p><!--[P4]--><!--[AM4]--><span id="AM4"></span><DIV class="marketing-INLINE_SUBSCRIPTION marketing-content" data-marketing-id="INLINE_SUBSCRIPTION"><div class="inline-subscription mb-30 mx-sm-50 text-center">
Get exclusive access to content from our 1768 First Edition with your subscription. 
<a href="https://subscription.britannica.com/subscribe?partnerCode=BP_House_USD_C" class="cta">Subscribe today</a>
</div></DIV><span id="MOD4"></span>
<p>The so-called King Movement was a response to the increasing threat to the Maori land. In 1857 several tribes of the Waikato area of North Island elected as king Te Wherowhero, who reigned as <span id="ref180282"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Potatau-I" class="md-crosslink">Potatau I</a>. In addition to electing a king, they established a council of state, a <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/judiciary" class="md-crosslink autoxref">judicial system</a>, and a police organization, all of which were intended to support Maori resolve to retain their land and to stop the intertribal warfare over the issue. Not all Maori accepted the authority of the king, but the majority shared with the King Movement the resolve not to sell the land.</p><!--[P5]--><div class="assemblies"><figure class="md-assembly  print-false" data-assembly-id="154960"><div class="md-assembly-wrapper" data-type="image"><div class="image-wrapper mg"><a href="https://cdn.britannica.com/53/146853-050-82300522/Potatau-Te-Wherowhero-Tawhiao-Tukaroto-Matutaera-Maori.jpg" class="media-overlay-link" data-href="/media/1/363450/154960"><img loading="lazy" src="https://cdn.britannica.com/s:700x500/53/146853-050-82300522/Potatau-Te-Wherowhero-Tawhiao-Tukaroto-Matutaera-Maori.jpg" alt="Tukaroto Matutaera Potatau Te Wherowhero Tawhiao, the second Maori king (1860–94)." data-width="1187" data-height="1600"></a></div></div><figcaption><span class="md-assembly-caption">Tukaroto Matutaera Potatau Te Wherowhero Tawhiao, the second Maori king (1860–94).</span><cite>Frank and Frances Carpenter Collection/Library of Congress, Washington, D.C. (LC-USZ62-109768)</cite></figcaption></figure></div><!--[AM5]--><div style="width:300px; height:250px" class="inline-ad" id="div-gpt-ad-d5f5f45d-cf67-48fe-8452-bc90aa3f2ac5" data-info='{"tagName":"DESKTOP","divId":"d5f5f45d-cf67-48fe-8452-bc90aa3f2ac5","pos":"MID2","desktopSize":{"width":300,"height":250}}' data-targeting = '{"ARTICLE":"363450","CAT":"history society,social sciences","TEMPLATE":"1"}'><script>googletag.cmd.push( function() { googletag.display('div-gpt-ad-d5f5f45d-cf67-48fe-8452-bc90aa3f2ac5'); } );</script></div><span id="AM5"></span><!--[MOD5]--><span id="MOD5"></span>
<p>Until 1860 the Maori still owned most of the land of North Island, but a large increase in the number of immigrants in the 1850s led to demands for greatly increased land purchase by the government. Many Maori were determined not to sell. In 1859 Te Teira, a Maori of the Taranaki area, sold his Waitara River land to the colonial government without the consent of his tribe, precipitating the <span id="ref180283"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/event/First-Taranaki-War" class="md-crosslink">First Taranaki War</a> (1860–61). Only the extremist wing of the King Movement joined in the First Taranaki War.</p><!--[P6]--><!--[AM6]--><span id="AM6"></span><!--[MOD6]--><span id="MOD6"></span></section>
<!--[H4]--><section id="ref283777" data-level="1"><h2 class="h1">Maori versus <em>pakeha</em></h2>
<p>The war consisted essentially of a series of generally successful sieges of <span id="ref180284"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Maori-Wars" class="md-crosslink">Maori</a> <em><span id="ref794787"></span>pa</em>s (fortified villages) by British troops and militia. The British were defeated during an attack (June 1860) on <span id="ref180285"></span>Puketakauere <em>pa</em> when the Maori executed a surprise counterattack, but the Maori were defeated at Orongomai in October and Mahoetahi in November. The war ended in a truce after the surrender of the Te Arei <em>pa</em> in late March 1861. The Maori remained in possession of the European-owned Tataraimaka block of land.</p><!--[P7]--><!--[AM7]--><span id="AM7"></span><!--[MOD7]--><span id="MOD7"></span>
<p>The fighting resumed in the <span id="ref794794"></span>Second Taranaki War in April 1863 after Governor Grey built an attack road into the Waikato area and drove the Taranaki Maori from the <span id="ref180286"></span>Tataraimaka block. While fighting raged in Taranaki once again, the <span id="ref180287"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Waikato-War" class="md-crosslink">Waikato War</a> began in July 1863, and the <a href="https://www.britannica.com/place/Waikato-River" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Waikato River</a> region, the centre of the King Movement tribes, became the main target of the Europeans. Once again the war was decided by sieges of Maori <em>pa</em>s, but the Maori also began to employ guerrilla tactics. British troops were aided by gunboats and forest ranger units made up of colonial volunteers. The Europeans won notable victories at Meremere in October 1863 and at Rangiriri in November. The fall of the Orakau <em>pa</em> in early April 1864 essentially brought the Waikato War to an end.</p><!--[P8]--><!--[AM8]--><span id="AM8"></span><!--[MOD8]--><span id="MOD8"></span>
<p>The last of the wars—known to the Europeans as “the fire in the fern” and to the Maori as <em><span id="ref180288"></span>te riri pakeha</em>, “the white <a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Mien" class="md-crosslink autoxref">man’s</a> anger,”—was fought from 1864 to 1872. Hostilities spread to virtually the whole of North Island. The main Maori combatants in the mid-60s were the fanatic <span id="ref180289"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hauhau" class="md-crosslink">Hauhau</a> warriors. The British government wanted to conclude peace in 1864, but the colonial government, wishing to acquire more land, continued the war and assumed an increasing share of the fighting. In July 1865 Grey led the capture of Weroroa <em>pa</em> in southern Taranaki. European and supporting Maori forces (increasingly numerous after 1864) checked each new effort by the King Movement tribes. From 1868 to 1872, the Hauhau were supplemented by members of a new warrior cult, <span id="ref180290"></span><a href="https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ringatu" class="md-crosslink">Ringatu</a>, founded and led by a guerrilla leader, <a href="https://www.britannica.com/biography/Te-Kooti" class="md-crosslink autoxref">Te Kooti</a>.</p><!--[P9]--><!--[AM9]--><span id="AM9"></span><!--[MOD9]--><span id="MOD9"></span></section>
